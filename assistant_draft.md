# From Telecommunication to Healthcare: How I Found My Place in AI

## Introduction

My name is Akhila Swaminathan Pugazhendhi. I am currently pursuing my Master's degree at Morgan State University, building on a Bachelor's in Computer Science and Engineering. Before coming back to school, I spent five years working in telecommunications as a software tester diving deep into network systems, catching bugs before they became problems, and learning how complex infrastructure really works from the inside. These days I also work as a Graduate Assistant in web development, and I had the chance to volunteer as a Data Analyst at the University of Maryland, where I got hands-on with real datasets and learned how much good analysis can actually change decisions.

## Industry Focus

The two industries I keep coming back to are healthcare and telecommunications. Telecom shaped how I think — I lived it for five years and I know how much people depend on reliable connectivity for everything from running a business to calling for help in an emergency. Healthcare pulls me in for a different reason: it is deeply personal. Technology in healthcare is not just about efficiency, it is about whether someone gets the right diagnosis in time, whether a doctor has the information they need, whether a patient feels seen. These are the kinds of problems I want to work on.

## Three Ways Generative AI Can Make a Real Difference

- In healthcare, generative AI can help doctors make faster and more informed decisions by pulling together patient history, symptoms, and recent research to suggest possible diagnoses — giving clinicians more time to focus on the patient in front of them rather than paperwork.

- In telecommunications, generative AI can monitor network behavior around the clock and describe failures or anomalies in plain language the moment they happen, so engineers can respond before customers even notice something is wrong.

- Across both industries, generative AI can turn mountains of raw data into clear, readable reports and documentation in minutes — work that used to take hours and often got skipped entirely because there simply was not enough time.
